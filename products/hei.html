Home Equity Investment

A Home Equity Investment is a modern alternative to traditional home equity loans and HELOCs. You get a lump sum of cash today in exchange for a share of your home's future appreciation — no monthly payments, no interest charges, and no added debt on your credit report. When you eventually sell, refinance, or reach the end of the term, you settle up based on your home's value at that time.

How HEI works

You receive a lump sum today (typically 10-20% of your home's current value) in exchange for an agreed share of your home's future appreciation. There are no monthly payments and no interest during the term. When you sell, refinance, or reach the end of the term (typically 10-30 years), you pay back the original amount plus the investor's share of appreciation. If home values decline, the investor typically shares in the loss too — protecting you from downside risk.

Who it's best for

  • Homeowners who can't qualify for traditional HELOCs due to DTI or income
  • Self-employed business owners with variable income
  • Retirees with equity but limited W-2 income
  • Anyone who wants capital without adding monthly debt payments
  • Short-term capital needs when you plan to sell within the term

Requirements

  • Primary residence, second home, or investment property
  • Minimum home value typically $250,000+
  • At least 20-25% equity in the property
  • Credit score of 500+ (much more flexible than HELOCs)
  • No minimum income requirement on most programs
  • Home appraisal and title review

Advantages

  • Zero monthly payments — doesn't affect cash flow
  • No interest charges during the term
  • More flexible qualification than HELOCs or cash-out refis
  • Not reported as debt on most credit bureaus
  • Investor shares downside risk if home value declines
  • Ideal for self-employed and variable-income borrowers

Frequently asked questions

Reverse mortgages are typically only available to homeowners 62+ and accrue interest over time, reducing your equity every month. HEIs are available at any age, don't accrue interest, and let you settle up on your terms — when you sell, refinance, or at the end of the term.

It varies by provider and deal structure, but typically ranges from 10% to 60% of future appreciation, depending on how much cash you take upfront relative to your home's value. Your specialist will walk you through each offer so you understand the exact terms.

Most HEI programs share in the downside — meaning you owe less than the original amount you received if your home appraises lower at settlement. This is one of the key advantages over a traditional loan, which you'd owe in full regardless of market conditions.

There are three typical triggers: you sell the home (settlement from sale proceeds), you refinance and pay off the HEI, or you reach the end of the term (typically 10-30 years). Some programs allow partial early repayment. There are no prepayment penalties on most offerings.
