How Invoice Factoring Works:
A Complete Guide for Small Businesses

You've done the work, sent the invoice, and now you're waiting — 30, 60, sometimes 90 days — for a client to pay. Meanwhile, payroll is due, suppliers need payment, and your next project requires materials you can't afford. Invoice factoring exists to close exactly this gap: turn outstanding invoices into cash today, without taking on debt.

What Is Invoice Factoring?

Invoice factoring is the sale of outstanding invoices to a third-party company (called a "factor") at a discount, in exchange for an immediate cash advance. The factor then collects payment from your clients when the invoices come due.

Key distinction: this is not a loan. You're selling a business asset (a receivable) at a slight discount to get cash now. There's no interest rate, no debt on your balance sheet, and no monthly loan payments.

Invoice factoring is particularly common in industries with long payment cycles: staffing, trucking and freight, construction, manufacturing, wholesale distribution, and professional services.

How the Factoring Process Works

  1. You deliver goods or services and issue an invoice to your client (net-30, net-60, or net-90 terms).
  2. You submit the invoice to your factor along with verification that the work was completed.
  3. The factor advances 70–95% of the invoice face value — typically within 24–48 hours.
  4. Your client pays the factor directly when the invoice comes due.
  5. The factor releases the remaining balance (the reserve) to you, minus their factoring fee.

Example

You have a $100,000 invoice due in 60 days. Your factor advances 85% immediately: $85,000 hits your account within 24 hours. The factor charges a 2.5% fee for the 60-day period ($2,500). When your client pays the full $100,000, the factor releases the remaining $12,500 ($15,000 reserve minus $2,500 fee) to you. Total cost: $2,500 to access $85,000 two months early.

Recourse vs. Non-Recourse Factoring

This is the most important distinction in factoring contracts — and it's frequently misunderstood.

Recourse factoring

If your client doesn't pay the invoice (for any reason), you are responsible for buying it back from the factor or replacing it with another invoice of equal value. You bear the credit risk. Recourse factoring is less expensive because the factor takes on less risk.

Non-recourse factoring

If your client doesn't pay because they've become insolvent or bankrupt, the factor absorbs that loss — you keep the advance. Note: "non-recourse" typically only covers insolvency, not disputes or chargebacks. It does not mean you have zero liability in every default scenario. Non-recourse factoring carries higher fees.

For most small businesses, recourse factoring with creditworthy clients is the better economic choice — you're unlikely to have major clients go bankrupt, and the lower fees add up over time.

What Does Invoice Factoring Cost?

Factoring fees typically range from 1% to 5% of the invoice value per 30-day period, depending on:

  • Your monthly invoice volume — larger volume typically earns lower rates
  • Your clients' creditworthiness — blue-chip clients mean lower risk and lower fees
  • Invoice payment terms — net-90 invoices cost more than net-30
  • Industry — some sectors carry higher default risk and higher fees
  • Recourse vs. non-recourse — non-recourse adds 0.5–1.5% to the fee

Who Qualifies for Invoice Factoring?

Factoring approval is primarily based on your clients' creditworthiness — not yours. This makes it accessible to businesses with challenged credit, short operating history, or prior financial difficulties.

Typical qualifications:

  • You invoice other businesses or government entities (B2B or B2G — not consumer invoices)
  • Your clients are creditworthy and pay reliably
  • Invoices are for completed work (factors won't advance on future work)
  • No UCC liens or legal claims on your receivables

Industries that factor frequently include: trucking, staffing, medical/healthcare, government contractors, wholesale, manufacturing, and IT/consulting services.

Benefits of Invoice Factoring

  • Immediate cash flow without waiting net-30/60/90 for payment
  • No debt created — factoring doesn't appear as a liability on your balance sheet
  • Credit score not the primary factor — your clients' payment history matters more
  • Scales with your business — as invoices grow, so does available funding
  • Collections management — many factors handle collections, freeing your time
  • No collateral beyond receivables — your equipment and real estate are not at risk

Drawbacks to Consider

  • Client relationships — clients will know you use a factor and pay a third party
  • Not available for consumer invoices — B2C businesses don't qualify
  • Long-term contracts — some factors require minimum volume commitments or multi-month agreements
  • Concentration limits — factors often won't advance on invoices where one client represents more than 25–30% of your total receivables

Frequently Asked Questions

Invoice factoring is when a business sells its outstanding invoices to a third-party company (a factor) at a discount in exchange for immediate cash. The factor then collects payment directly from your clients when the invoices come due. It is not a loan — it's a sale of receivables.

Invoice factoring involves selling your invoices outright — the factor collects payment from your clients. Invoice financing uses invoices as collateral for a loan — you retain ownership and collect payment yourself. Factoring is faster and doesn't require credit checks on your business, but your clients will know a third party is involved.

In most factoring arrangements, your clients pay the factor directly, so they will know you use factoring. Most reputable factors communicate professionally and do not damage client relationships. Some businesses prefer confidential arrangements — certain invoice financing structures can be set up so clients are unaware.

A factoring fee (also called a discount rate) is the cost charged by the factor, typically 1–5% of the invoice value per 30-day period. For example, a 3% fee on a $100,000 invoice means you pay $3,000 for the advance. Fees vary based on invoice volume, client creditworthiness, and payment terms.
