Business Loan Requirements:
What Lenders Actually Look For

Most guides on business loan requirements tell you the same thing: good credit, two years in business, solid revenue. That's the checklist — but it's not how underwriters actually think. Understanding the real logic behind loan decisions lets you present your application in the best possible light and avoid the mistakes that cause unnecessary declines.

The Two Worlds of Business Lending

Business lending requirements vary dramatically depending on whether you're applying to a traditional bank/SBA lender or an alternative lender. The same business that gets declined by a bank can be approved and funded by an alternative lender the same day — not because one is less rigorous, but because they're evaluating different signals.

What Every Lender Evaluates: The 5 Cs of Credit

Banks and alternative lenders both use some version of the "5 Cs" framework, though they weight each differently.

1. Character

Character is your credit history — personal and business. It tells the lender whether you've honored your financial obligations in the past. For traditional lenders, this means a thorough review of your personal credit report, business credit report (Dun & Bradstreet, Experian Business), payment history, and any public records (judgments, liens, bankruptcies). For alternative lenders, character is a lighter check — they look at your score as a rough signal, not a precise predictor.

2. Capacity

Capacity is your ability to repay — the most important factor for most lenders. For traditional lenders, this means a DSCR (debt service coverage ratio) analysis: do you generate enough income to cover the new payment? For alternative lenders, capacity is assessed through bank statement analysis: average monthly deposits, average daily balance, and consistency of cash flow.

A common threshold: most lenders want to see that the monthly loan payment doesn't exceed 15–20% of your average monthly revenue. If you're bringing in $50,000/month and applying for a loan with a $12,000 monthly payment, that's a 24% debt service burden — likely too high.

3. Capital

Capital refers to how much you've personally invested in the business and what financial reserves you have. Traditional lenders want to see that owners have "skin in the game" — you're less likely to walk away from a business you've put $200,000 into than one you started on $5,000. Alternative lenders care less about invested capital and more about current financial health.

4. Collateral

Collateral is assets pledged to secure the loan. Traditional bank loans often require collateral — real estate, equipment, inventory, or a blanket lien on business assets. SBA loans require a personal guarantee from all owners with 20%+ stake. Most alternative loans are unsecured — no specific collateral required — though many include a UCC-1 filing (a general lien on business assets) as a backstop.

5. Conditions

Conditions refers to the purpose of the loan, the state of your industry, and the broader economic environment. A loan to fund a proven opportunity in a stable industry is viewed differently than a loan to a struggling business in a declining sector. Be prepared to explain exactly what you're using the capital for — vague answers ("working capital") are weaker than specific ones ("funding 60 days of payroll while we wait for a $400K contract to close").

What Alternative Lenders Focus On

Alternative lenders streamline underwriting by focusing on the three data points most predictive of repayment for small businesses:

Bank statement analysis

The primary underwriting document for most alternative lenders. Underwriters look at:

  • Average monthly deposits — used to estimate revenue and size the loan offer
  • Average daily balance — indicates whether cash flow can support daily/weekly payments
  • Negative days — how many days per month the account went below $0
  • NSF frequency — non-sufficient funds events signal cash flow stress
  • Deposit consistency — regular deposits are better than erratic large deposits
  • Existing loan payments — visible in the statement; lenders calculate your existing debt load

Industry and business type

Certain industries are considered higher risk based on historical default rates: restaurants, retail, cannabis, adult entertainment, and some construction sectors face tighter terms or lower approval rates across most lenders. Healthcare, professional services, and established B2B businesses generally receive more favorable terms.

Time in business

The first year of business has the highest default rates. Most alternative lenders draw the line at 6 months; many prefer 12+. The longer your operating history, the lower your perceived risk and the better your terms.

Documents Typically Required

Alternative lenders (fast approval)

  • One-page application (business info, owner info, funding amount)
  • 3–6 months of business bank statements
  • Government-issued ID
  • Voided business check

Traditional banks and SBA loans

  • All of the above, plus:
  • 2 years of business tax returns
  • 2 years of personal tax returns (all 20%+ owners)
  • Current profit & loss statement (within 90 days)
  • Current balance sheet
  • Business plan with financial projections (for startups or large loan amounts)
  • List of collateral
  • Business licenses and formation documents
  • Accounts receivable/payable aging reports (for larger amounts)

Common Reasons Applications Get Declined

  • Excessive NSFs on bank statements — even with good revenue, 4+ NSFs per month flags cash management problems
  • Negative days — a balance that frequently hits zero or below signals inability to handle fixed payments
  • Recent bankruptcy — most lenders require 2+ years post-discharge; some alternative lenders work with 1+ year
  • Open tax liens — unresolved federal or state tax liens are a near-universal decline factor
  • Already stacking loans — multiple existing advances visible in bank statements raise debt service concerns
  • Industry restrictions — some lenders won't fund certain industries regardless of financial health
  • Inconsistent information — discrepancies between your application and bank statements

How to Strengthen Your Application Before Applying

  1. Clean up bank statements for 60–90 days — zero NSFs, positive balance every day
  2. Pay down revolving credit balances — get utilization below 30%
  3. Resolve any open tax liens — a payment plan with the IRS or state is better than no plan
  4. Know your numbers — be able to state your monthly revenue, gross margin, and existing debt payments without looking them up
  5. Have a clear use of funds — specific, revenue-connected purposes underwrite better than vague requests

Frequently Asked Questions

For alternative lenders, the typical documents required are: a completed application, 3–6 months of business bank statements, a government-issued ID, and proof of business ownership. Bank and SBA loans additionally require 2 years of business tax returns, 2 years of personal tax returns, a profit and loss statement, a balance sheet, and sometimes a business plan with financial projections.

Most alternative lenders require at least 6 months in business. Bank loans and SBA loans typically require 2+ years. Some revenue-based products like merchant cash advances may fund businesses as young as 3–4 months with strong monthly revenue.

DSCR measures your ability to cover debt payments from operating income. It's calculated as net operating income divided by total debt service. Most lenders want a DSCR of 1.25 or higher — meaning you generate $1.25 in operating income for every $1.00 of debt payment. A DSCR below 1.0 means you don't generate enough income to cover existing obligations, which typically results in a decline.
