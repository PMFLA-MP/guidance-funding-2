What Is a Merchant Cash Advance?
Pros, Cons, and When to Use One

A merchant cash advance is one of the most misunderstood funding products in small business finance. It's not a loan, it doesn't have a fixed monthly payment, and it's not priced in APR. Understanding exactly how it works — and when it's the right tool — can save you from expensive missteps or help you access capital when nothing else will.

What Is a Merchant Cash Advance?

A merchant cash advance (MCA) is a purchase of a portion of your future business revenue in exchange for an upfront lump sum today. The MCA provider gives you cash now; in return, they collect a fixed percentage of your daily or weekly revenue until the agreed total repayment amount is reached.

Because it's structured as a revenue purchase rather than a loan, MCAs operate outside traditional lending regulations. There's no fixed interest rate, no set term length, and no traditional amortization schedule. The repayment adjusts with your revenue — faster in good months, slower in slow months.

How Does an MCA Work?

Here's a step-by-step walkthrough of a typical merchant cash advance:

  1. You apply with 3–6 months of business bank statements (or credit card processing statements). Most providers have a 1–2 page application.
  2. The provider underwrites based on your average monthly deposits or card volume — not your credit score.
  3. You receive an offer that specifies: the advance amount, the factor rate, the total repayment amount, and the holdback percentage.
  4. Funds are deposited into your business account within 24–48 hours of approval.
  5. Daily or weekly repayments are automatically debited from your account — either as a percentage of card transactions (split-withholding) or as a fixed ACH debit from your bank account.

Key MCA Terms Explained

Factor rate

The factor rate is the cost multiplier applied to your advance. It's expressed as a decimal: 1.1, 1.25, 1.4, etc. Multiply the advance amount by the factor rate to get your total repayment obligation.

Example: $60,000 advance × 1.35 factor rate = $81,000 total repayment. Your cost of capital is $21,000.

Holdback percentage

The holdback (also called the retrieval rate) is the percentage of daily revenue withheld to repay the advance. Common holdback rates are 10–20% of daily card volume or bank deposits. Higher holdback = faster repayment = shorter effective term = higher APR. Lower holdback = slower repayment = lower effective APR.

Effective APR

MCAs don't quote APR — they quote factor rates. But the effective APR depends on how quickly you repay. A 1.3 factor rate repaid in 4 months has a much higher APR than the same factor rate repaid in 14 months. This is why comparing MCA factor rates to loan interest rates isn't straightforward.

What Does an MCA Actually Cost?

Let's run real numbers. You take a $50,000 MCA with a 1.35 factor rate and a 15% holdback on $80,000/month in revenue:

  • Total repayment: $50,000 × 1.35 = $67,500
  • Cost of capital: $17,500
  • Daily holdback (assuming 22 business days/month): $80,000 × 15% ÷ 22 = ~$545/day
  • Estimated repayment time: $67,500 ÷ $545 ≈ ~124 business days (~5.6 months)
  • Effective APR: approximately 60–75%

That's expensive compared to a bank loan — but if a bank won't approve you, the comparison is irrelevant. The real question is: does the business opportunity you're funding justify the cost?

Pros of a Merchant Cash Advance

  • Speed: Funding in 24–48 hours — often the fastest capital available to small businesses.
  • Minimal credit requirements: Revenue matters more than credit score. Scores as low as 500 are workable.
  • No fixed payment: Repayment scales with revenue — you pay less in slow months, more in strong months.
  • No collateral required: MCAs are unsecured. Your business assets are not at risk.
  • Simple approval process: Typically just bank statements and a short application. No tax returns, no business plan.
  • No prepayment benefit: If your business surges and you repay early, your total repayment amount doesn't change — though this also means no prepayment penalty.

Cons of a Merchant Cash Advance

  • High cost of capital: Effective APRs of 40–150% are common. MCAs are among the most expensive business financing products available.
  • Daily cash flow impact: Automatic daily debits can strain businesses with tight cash flow or irregular deposit patterns.
  • No credit bureau reporting: Repaying an MCA on time does not build your business credit history.
  • Stacking risk: Taking multiple simultaneous MCAs (called stacking) is heavily scrutinized by lenders and can trap businesses in a cycle of debt.
  • Unregulated industry: Because MCAs aren't loans, disclosure requirements are minimal. Some providers obscure total costs — always calculate the factor rate yourself.

When an MCA Makes Sense

Despite the cost, MCAs are the right tool in specific situations:

  • You need capital immediately and can't wait 30–90 days for a bank or SBA loan.
  • You have a high-margin opportunity that will more than cover the cost of the advance — a large inventory purchase before peak season, a contract that requires upfront materials, or an equipment need that unlocks higher revenue.
  • Your credit prevents other options — MCAs are one of the few products accessible to businesses with scores under 550.
  • Your revenue is seasonal or variable and you need a payment that flexes with cash flow rather than a fixed monthly obligation.
  • You've been turned down elsewhere and the alternative is missed payroll, a lapsed contract, or lost business.

When to Avoid an MCA

MCAs are the wrong tool when:

  • You qualify for a term loan or SBA loan — the cost difference is substantial.
  • Your margins are thin and the daily holdback would put you in a negative cash position.
  • You're already servicing one or more MCAs — stacking compounds the cost and risk significantly.
  • The use of funds is non-revenue-generating (paying off debt, covering personal expenses).

MCA vs. Other Short-Term Funding Options

Before committing to an MCA, consider whether these alternatives might work:

  • Invoice factoring — If you have outstanding invoices, factoring can advance 70–90% of their value at much lower cost than an MCA.
  • Short-term term loan — If your credit is 600+, a 6–18 month term loan may cost significantly less.
  • Purchase order financing — If the capital is for fulfilling a specific order, PO financing is purpose-built and typically cheaper.
