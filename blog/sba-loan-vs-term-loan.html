SBA Loan vs. Term Loan:
Which Is Right for Your Business?

Both SBA loans and term loans put a lump sum in your account with fixed monthly payments. That's where the similarity ends. The funding process, approval timeline, interest rates, documentation requirements, and use cases are entirely different — and choosing the wrong one can cost you weeks or tens of thousands of dollars in unnecessary interest.

What Is an SBA Loan?

An SBA loan isn't directly issued by the Small Business Administration — it's a bank loan that the SBA partially guarantees (typically 75–85%). That guarantee lets approved banks offer lower rates and longer terms than they otherwise would for small business borrowers. The tradeoff: the SBA's guarantee program comes with strict eligibility rules, heavy documentation requirements, and a long approval process.

The most common SBA programs are:

  • SBA 7(a) — General-purpose loans up to $5 million for working capital, equipment, real estate, or acquisition
  • SBA 504 — Real estate and major equipment, structured as two loans (bank + SBA debenture)
  • SBA Express — Expedited SBA 7(a) up to $500K with a 36-hour SBA response guarantee
  • SBA Microloan — Up to $50K through nonprofit intermediary lenders

What Is a Term Loan?

A term loan is a direct loan from a bank, credit union, or alternative lender — no government guarantee involved. You borrow a fixed amount, repay it over a set term (typically 1–5 years for alternative lenders, up to 10+ years for bank products) with fixed or variable interest, and the lender sets their own underwriting criteria.

Alternative lender term loans have streamlined significantly in recent years — many can approve and fund within 24–72 hours using automated underwriting based on bank statements and credit data rather than full business financials.

When to Choose an SBA Loan

SBA loans are the right choice when:

  • You need a large amount ($250K+) and want the lowest possible long-term cost
  • You're buying real estate, acquiring a business, or making a major capital investment
  • You have 2+ years in business, solid financials, and can wait 60–90 days for funding
  • You want the longest possible repayment terms to keep monthly payments low
  • You have time to assemble extensive documentation (tax returns, P&Ls, business plan, projections)

When to Choose a Term Loan

Alternative term loans make more sense when:

  • You need capital quickly — within days, not months
  • Your business is under 2 years old
  • You can't produce 2+ years of tax returns
  • The higher rate is justified by the opportunity cost of waiting (a contract, inventory purchase, or time-sensitive expansion)
  • You want a simpler, faster application process

The Real Cost Comparison

The rate difference between SBA and alternative term loans is real — but so is the opportunity cost of waiting. Consider a business that needs $200K for a contract that generates $80K in profit over 6 months:

  • SBA loan at 12% APR: Wait 75 days. Miss the contract. Net gain: $0.
  • Alternative term loan at 30% APR over 18 months: Fund in 48 hours. Cost of capital: ~$45K. Net gain: $35K.

The "expensive" loan was worth it. The math changes for a $500K equipment purchase over 10 years — there, the SBA wins decisively. Always evaluate cost in the context of use, not in isolation.

Can You Get Both?

Yes — and it's a common strategy. Many businesses use an alternative term loan to bridge a short-term need while simultaneously applying for an SBA loan for a longer-term capital project. Just be mindful that having open loan balances affects your SBA debt service coverage calculation.

Which One Is Right for You?

There's no universal answer — it depends on your timeline, use of funds, credit profile, and risk tolerance. Frequently Asked Questions

Most SBA 7(a) loans take 30–90 days from application to funding, depending on the lender, loan size, and documentation completeness. SBA Express loans have a 36-hour SBA response guarantee but still typically take 2–4 weeks to fund after approval.

SBA loans typically offer lower interest rates and longer repayment terms. However, they require more documentation, have stricter eligibility, and take significantly longer to fund. For businesses that qualify and have time to wait, SBA loans are usually the better long-term value. For urgent capital needs, alternative term loans are the practical choice.

Alternative lender term loans are available to businesses with credit scores as low as 600. Bank term loans and SBA-guaranteed term loans typically require 680 or higher. The specific minimum depends on the lender and loan amount.
