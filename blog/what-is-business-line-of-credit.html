What Is a Business Line of Credit
and How Does It Work?

A business line of credit is one of the most versatile financial tools available to small business owners — but it's also one of the most misunderstood. Unlike a loan, you don't receive a lump sum. Unlike a credit card, you can access large amounts at much lower rates. Understanding exactly how it works, when to use it, and how it compares to other options is the key to using it effectively.

What Is a Business Line of Credit?

A business line of credit is a revolving credit facility that gives you access to a set credit limit. You draw funds as needed, repay them, and the available credit replenishes. You only pay interest on the amount you've drawn, not the full limit. Think of it like a large, flexible reservoir of capital that you tap when needed and refill as you repay.

Lines of credit are ideal for managing cash flow gaps, covering short-term working capital needs, funding recurring expenses during slow seasons, and having a financial safety net without paying for money you're not using.

How a Business Line of Credit Works

  1. You're approved for a credit limit — typically $10,000 to $500,000 for small businesses, based on your revenue, credit history, and time in business.
  2. You draw funds when needed — via online transfer, check, or debit card linked to the line.
  3. Interest accrues only on your balance — if you draw $20,000 from a $100,000 line, you pay interest on $20,000.
  4. You repay on a set schedule — minimum payments are usually interest-only or a small percentage of the balance; paying down principal restores available credit.
  5. The line renews — most lines have annual renewal reviews; lenders assess your financials and renew the line, adjust the limit, or close it.

Types of Business Lines of Credit

Unsecured line of credit

No collateral required. Approval is based on creditworthiness, revenue, and time in business. Unsecured lines typically have higher interest rates than secured lines because the lender has no asset to recover if you default. Most small business lines of credit from online lenders are unsecured.

Secured line of credit

Backed by collateral — typically accounts receivable, inventory, or a blanket lien on business assets. Lower rates than unsecured lines because the lender's risk is reduced. Banks often require secured lines for larger credit limits ($250K+).

Asset-based line of credit (ABL)

Borrowing capacity is tied directly to the value of specific collateral, usually accounts receivable or inventory. As your receivables or inventory grows, your available credit grows. As you collect or sell, the line decreases. ABLs are common for manufacturers, distributors, and businesses with significant working capital tied up in inventory or receivables.

Revenue-based line of credit

Newer fintech products that size your line based on card processing volume or bank deposit history rather than traditional underwriting. No fixed credit limit — it adjusts dynamically with your business performance. Faster to obtain and more accessible to newer businesses, but often at higher rates than bank lines.

Interest Rates on Business Lines of Credit

Rates vary widely by lender type:

  • Bank lines of credit: Prime rate + 1–4%, typically 8–14% APR in the current rate environment
  • Credit union lines: Similar to banks, sometimes slightly lower
  • Online / alternative lender lines: 15–40% APR, sometimes higher for shorter-term or lower-credit products
  • Revenue-based lines: Often priced as a weekly fee (e.g., 1.5–3% per week on outstanding balance) rather than APR

In addition to interest, lines of credit often have: annual fees ($100–$500), draw fees (1–3% per draw), inactivity fees (if you don't use the line), and maintenance fees. Always calculate the total cost including fees, not just the stated interest rate.

Best Uses for a Business Line of Credit

  • Managing seasonal cash flow — a retailer bridging the gap between slow summer months and the holiday surge
  • Covering payroll during a revenue gap — when a large client is late paying or a project is delayed
  • Taking advantage of supplier discounts — paying invoices early for 2–3% discounts that exceed the cost of borrowing
  • Funding recurring business expenses — marketing campaigns, inventory purchases, or contract labor that generates predictable revenue
  • Emergency reserve — an available line you don't draw unless needed, giving peace of mind without ongoing interest cost

What a Business Line of Credit Is Not Good For

  • Large one-time capital needs — buying equipment or real estate is better served by a term loan or equipment financing at lower rates
  • Long-term capital investment — using a revolving line for assets with 10-year useful lives creates mismatched repayment risk
  • Debt consolidation — revolving debt can easily balloon if minimum payments are all that's made; better to use a term loan for defined payoff

How to Qualify for a Business Line of Credit

Requirements vary significantly by lender type:

  • Bank / credit union: 680+ credit score, 2+ years in business, $250K+ annual revenue, financials on hand
  • Online lender: 600+ credit score, 12 months in business, $100K+ annual revenue, 3 months bank statements
  • Revenue-based line: $10K–$15K/month in deposits or card volume, 3–6 months in business, minimal credit requirements
