How Fast Can You Get a Business Loan?
(By Product Type)

When someone asks "how fast can I get a business loan?" the honest answer is: it depends entirely on what type of loan you're getting. The fastest business funding products can deposit money in your account within hours. The slowest take three months or more. Knowing the timeline before you apply is the difference between solving your cash flow problem and missing the window entirely.

Same-Day and 24-Hour Funding

Merchant Cash Advance

The merchant cash advance is the fastest business funding product available. Applications require only bank statements and a short form. Automated underwriting systems can return decisions in under an hour, and funds typically arrive same-day or next-day via ACH. Same-day funding is available for applications submitted before noon ET with a clean bank statement and no follow-up items needed.

Best for: Businesses that need capital immediately and have consistent monthly revenue, even with challenged credit.

Short-term revenue-based loans

Similar to MCAs in speed, short-term loans (3–18 month terms) from alternative lenders are largely automated. Many fintech lenders integrate directly with business bank accounts and accounting platforms for near-instant underwriting. Same-day approvals are common; same-day funding depends on bank cut-off times.

24–72 Hour Funding

Invoice Factoring

For invoice factoring, the first transaction takes 24–72 hours to set up (factor verifies your clients, establishes the relationship). After that, subsequent invoice advances often process in 24 hours or less. If you have a $200,000 invoice from a Fortune 500 company, you can often have 85% of it ($170,000) in your account within two business days.

Alternative Term Loans

Alternative term loans ($10K–$500K) typically take 1–3 business days from application to funding. The application is light (bank statements + short form), decisions are largely automated, and ACH funding is next-business-day once approved. Larger amounts or applications with questions from underwriters take longer.

Equipment Financing

Equipment financing takes slightly longer because the lender needs to verify the equipment being purchased — a quote or invoice from the vendor, equipment type, and age. Most equipment loans fund within 2–5 days. If the equipment is used and requires an appraisal, add a few more days.

1–4 Week Funding

SBA Express Loans

SBA Express loans offer a 36-hour turnaround for the SBA's guarantee decision — not the full funding timeline. After the SBA responds, the lender still needs to finalize underwriting, prepare loan documents, and fund. Realistically, SBA Express loans take 2–4 weeks from application to funding. Still faster than a standard 7(a), but not remotely as fast as the name implies.

Bank Term Loans

Traditional bank term loans involve a full credit file review, sometimes appraisals, committee approval, and document preparation. Well-prepared applications to relationship banks can close in 2–3 weeks; a new application to an unfamiliar bank often takes 4–6 weeks.

30–90 Day Funding

SBA 7(a) Loans

Standard SBA 7(a) loans involve SBA review on top of the bank's underwriting process. The SBA itself takes 5–10 business days for its guarantee review. Add bank underwriting (2–4 weeks), document preparation, and closing, and a well-run SBA 7(a) takes 45–75 days. Complex transactions, real estate, or high-volume SBA lenders can push this to 90 days.

Commercial Mortgages

Commercial real estate transactions require property appraisals (2–3 weeks minimum), environmental assessments, title searches, and legal document preparation. Expect 45–90 days minimum from application to closing — and 120 days is not unusual for complex transactions.

What Slows Down Funding

Even fast products can get delayed. The most common causes:

  • Missing or incomplete documents — every follow-up adds 24–48 hours
  • Bank statement issues — NSFs, large unexplained deposits, or balance volatility trigger manual review
  • Existing UCC liens — if another lender has a lien on your business assets, the new lender needs that resolved or subordinated
  • Stale documents — P&Ls, balance sheets, and bank statements older than 90 days often need to be updated
  • Incomplete ownership disclosure — SBA and bank loans require all owners with 20%+ stake to sign personal guarantees and provide tax returns
