Equipment Financing vs. Equipment Leasing:
Key Differences

You need a piece of equipment — a truck, a CNC machine, a restaurant hood system, a medical device. You don't want to spend $150,000 in cash. Both equipment financing and equipment leasing let you use the equipment and pay over time, but ownership, tax treatment, flexibility, and total cost are fundamentally different between the two structures.

What Is Equipment Financing?

Equipment financing is a loan where the equipment itself serves as collateral. You borrow money to purchase the equipment outright, make fixed monthly payments over a set term (typically 2–7 years), and own the equipment free and clear once the loan is paid off. The lender holds a lien on the equipment until the balance is fully repaid.

Because the equipment is the collateral, equipment financing often has more favorable terms and lower credit requirements than unsecured loans. If you default, the lender repossesses the equipment — their risk is limited to the asset's value.

What Is Equipment Leasing?

Equipment leasing is a rental agreement where you pay to use equipment over a defined period without owning it. At the end of the lease, you typically have three options: return the equipment, renew the lease, or purchase the equipment at fair market value (or a predetermined amount, depending on lease type).

Leasing works well when equipment becomes obsolete quickly, when you prefer predictable operating expenses, or when you want to preserve capital and credit lines for other uses.

Types of Equipment Leases

Not all leases are created equal. Understanding the lease type changes the analysis significantly.

Operating lease (true lease)

The lessor retains ownership and the tax benefits of ownership. You pay for use. At the end, you return the equipment or renew. Monthly payments are lowest because you're not paying for the full asset value. The equipment stays off your balance sheet (though new accounting standards require most leases to appear as right-of-use assets).

Finance lease ($1 buyout or capital lease)

Structured like financing — you make payments over the term and purchase the equipment for $1 (or a nominal amount) at the end. The IRS treats this as a purchase, so you get depreciation benefits. Payments are higher than a true operating lease because you're paying for the full value of the equipment over the term. For practical purposes, a $1 buyout lease is functionally identical to equipment financing.

Fair market value (FMV) lease

You pay for use of the equipment, and at the end of the lease, you can buy it at its fair market value at that time. This gives you the option to upgrade if the equipment has depreciated significantly, or purchase if it still has strong value. Lower payments than a $1 buyout lease; higher total cost if you end up purchasing at FMV.

Tax Implications

Tax treatment is one of the most meaningful differences between financing and leasing. Consult your CPA for specifics — the rules change based on equipment type, business structure, and current tax law — but here's the general framework:

  • Equipment financing: You can deduct interest on the loan and depreciate the asset over its useful life. Under Section 179 of the tax code, you may be able to expense the full cost of the equipment in the year of purchase (up to the annual limit). Bonus depreciation rules can further accelerate deductions.
  • Operating lease: Lease payments are typically fully deductible as a business expense in the year paid. No depreciation deduction (you don't own the asset), but also no depreciation tracking required.
  • Finance / $1 buyout lease: Treated as a purchase for tax purposes — same depreciation and Section 179 treatment as financing.

When to Finance Equipment

Equipment financing is typically the better choice when:

  • The equipment has a long useful life (7+ years) and won't become obsolete quickly
  • The equipment holds or appreciates in value (real estate, certain industrial machinery)
  • You want to own the asset outright and benefit from its residual value
  • Section 179 expensing makes sense for your tax situation in the current year
  • You plan to use the equipment beyond the loan term — once it's paid off, operating cost drops to maintenance only

When to Lease Equipment

Leasing makes more sense when:

  • Technology or equipment evolves quickly and you want to upgrade every 3–5 years (copiers, computers, medical equipment, software-dependent machinery)
  • Lower monthly payments are critical to cash flow
  • You want predictable operating expenses without maintenance responsibility (many leases include service agreements)
  • You want to preserve your credit line or collateral for other borrowing needs
  • Your business model involves cycling equipment regularly

A Practical Example

A restaurant group needs a $120,000 commercial kitchen system. If they finance it at 8% over 5 years, the monthly payment is approximately $2,433 and they own it outright after 60 payments. If they lease it with a $750/month operating lease and exercise an FMV purchase option of $18,000 at the end, total cost is roughly $63,000 over the lease — but they didn't build equity, and the FMV buyout pushes total cost higher if exercised.

In this case, financing wins if the equipment lasts 10+ years and the restaurant intends to own it long-term. Leasing wins if the kitchen technology is likely to need upgrading in 4–5 years or if the group needs to minimize monthly overhead while scaling.
